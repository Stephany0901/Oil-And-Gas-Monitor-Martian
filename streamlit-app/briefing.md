# Oil & Gas Briefing — July 20, 2026

*Run before Monday's US open. The quote feed still returns the Friday, July 17 close, so the movers below are unchanged from yesterday's briefing — they are Friday's moves, not new ones.*

## Market

The Hormuz risk premium is still the whole story. Brent settled Friday around $88.10/bbl and WTI around $82.49, up roughly 4.5% on the day and more than 13% on the week, after Kuwait reported an Iranian strike on a power and desalination plant and the US ran a seventh consecutive night of strikes. CENTCOM said Sunday it had carried out dozens of further strikes aimed at degrading Iran's ability to hit vessels in the strait. Transit has thinned dramatically — ship-tracking counted nine vessels in the waterway over a twelve-hour window Saturday into Sunday, four of them Iranian-flagged. No weekend headline reversed the direction of travel.

The counterweight is unchanged. OPEC+ added another 188 kb/d for August on July 5 — a fifth consecutive monthly increase, roughly 800 kb/d of core-member quota additions since April — and the IEA has global supply rebounding to 98.8 mb/d in June. Most 2026 balances still point to surplus. Before the escalation WTI traded near five-month lows around $69. This is a geopolitical bid on a loose physical market, and it unwinds quickly if the corridor clears.

Natural gas remains the opposite trade: Henry Hub near $2.92/MMBtu, storage about 6.6% above the five-year average, cooler forecasts trimming cooling demand, and Freeport LNG maintenance reducing export pull. EIA's July STEO put the 2026 average at $3.67.

Refining is still where the earnings leverage sits — crack spreads near multi-decade highs, with refiners beating both crude and the broader energy index last week.

## Screen idea

Unchanged from yesterday, and the emptiness is still the finding. Of the 23 names checked, **only BKR closed below its 200-day average** ($55.95 vs $56.01) — and at 11.1x EV/EBITDA it is the most expensive of the cheap-looking cohort, so it fails the valuation half of the test. The genuinely cheap names — **DVN at 4.8x** and **OXY at 5.8x** — are both above their 200-day averages and fail the pullback half. No name satisfies both conditions. The closest approximation remains **HAL**: 8.6x EV/EBITDA and about 7% below its 50-day average, though still above its 200-day. More broadly, oilfield services (SLB at 46.99 vs a 50-day of 52.11; XES the weakest of the three ETFs) is conspicuously not participating while the E&Ps it serves make highs — worth understanding why before reading that gap as opportunity.
